# Generate Hypotheses

Produce ~30 **falsifiable** hypotheses from a single claim, point, or decision.
The danger is collapse: thirty near-duplicates restating one idea.
The defence is structure — six **angles**, each forced to yield distinct propositions.

Every hypothesis must be *falsifiable*: state what you would observe that would prove it wrong.
A proposition with no failure condition is an opinion, not a hypothesis — sharpen or discard it.

## Steps

### 1. Restate the claim

Put the claim, point, or decision into one crisp sentence: the proposition under test.
If it is not actually testable (a preference, a tautology), sharpen it into something evidence could confirm or refute before going on.

Completion: one sentence proposition, stated so that evidence could confirm or refute it.

### 2. Fill the six angles

Generate at least five hypotheses per angle.
Each angle takes a different stance toward the claim, so the set cannot collapse into restatements.

The angles — a flat peer-set, consult each as you fill it:

- **Assumptions** — what must already be true for the claim to hold; each is a load-bearing belief that could be false.
- **Failure modes** — how the claim could be wrong, or the decision backfire, in practice.
- **Boundary conditions** — where, when, or for whom the claim holds and where it breaks. Tilt toward the edges.
- **Alternatives** — rival claims that would explain the same observations; the claim has competition.
- **Predictions** — consequences that must follow if the claim is true; observable and checkable.
- **Counterfactuals** — what we would see if the claim were false. The world without it.

Completion: ≥5 falsifiable hypotheses per angle, each tagged to its angle.

### 3. De-duplicate and sharpen

Run the difference test across the whole set: does each hypothesis differ in *claim*, not just wording, from every other?
Merge or cut those that restate a sibling.
Then re-run the falsifiable test on what survives — every one needs a concrete failure condition.

Completion: 30 (±2) hypotheses, each falsifiable, each distinct in claim from the rest, each tagged to an angle.

### 4. Present

Return the hypotheses grouped by angle, numbered 1–30.
For each: one sentence for the proposition, one for what would refute it.

Completion: a grouped, numbered list of ~30, each with its refutation test.
